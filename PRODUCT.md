# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Prospective clients and collaborators visiting a design studio's portfolio — startup founders, creative directors, and brand managers evaluating creative partners. They arrive with high design literacy and low tolerance for generic agency tropes. Their job: determine within seconds whether this studio operates at the level they need.

## Product Purpose

A design studio portfolio that communicates capability through restraint. The site itself is the portfolio's strongest piece — every typographic and layout decision proves the studio's craft before a single project is shown. Success looks like a visitor thinking "if they care this much about their own site, imagine what they'd do for our brand."

## Brand Personality

Confident, restrained, architectural. Three words: **quiet authority**. Voice is direct and unadorned — no superlatives, no agency-speak. The interface trusts the visitor to recognize quality without being sold to. Emotional goal: the feeling of walking into a gallery with good lighting and generous wall space — calm, intentional, nothing fighting for attention.

## Anti-references

- Generic agency sites with carousels, gradient overlays, and "we help brands grow" copy
- Shouting hero sections with massive animated type and multiple CTAs
- Over-designed portfolios where the work is secondary to the chrome
- Cookie-cutter dark-mode templates (indigo accents, glassmorphism, floating 3D elements)
- Any site using stock photography of people in meetings or hands typing on laptops

## Design Principles

1. **Prove it, don't say it.** The site's own design is its best credential. Every layout decision, type choice, and spacing rhythm should demonstrate the craft the studio sells.

2. **Restraint is the identity.** Rationing color, motion, and ornament to exactly one moment per view creates more impact than filling every section. What you leave out is as intentional as what you include.

3. **Typography carries the weight.** With minimal color and no shadows, type does all the work — hierarchy, tone, rhythm. Every size, weight, and tracking choice is structural, not decorative.

4. **Gallery over dashboard.** Present work like art in a gallery: generous space, neutral container, let each piece breathe. The container should never compete with the content.

5. **Flat and architectural.** Surfaces separate by value, not by shadow. The interface is structural — like signage on a wall, not cards floating in space.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG AA compliance target
- Body text contrast must exceed 4.5:1 against #101010 canvas
- The amber accent (#e7c59a) must never be the sole carrier of information
- Reduced-motion alternative for any scroll-triggered transitions
- Focus indicators visible on dark backgrounds without relying on color alone
